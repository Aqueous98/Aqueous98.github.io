Iron Giant: Machine Vision

Machine vision is a topic of note because it is one of the many features of the Iron Giant. While machine vision is an emerging technology in the real world, the machine vision technology within the Iron Giant is highly advanced. The Iron Giant’s machine vision features facial recognition, weapon recognition, and targeting.

The Iron Giant’s facial recognition is quite accurate because it is able to determine who Hogarth is along with other characters within the movie. This is on par with the level of facial recognition technology that we have today. In 2014, facebook’s “DeepFace” algorithms had a facial recognition accuracy of 97.25% accuracy. Regarding the Iron Giant’s weapon recognition, it is able to accurately able to detect a weapon to which it then goes into a defense mode and tries to protect himself. Compared to the real world, the Iron Giant’s weapon detection technology would rival that of PatriotOne Technologies, a weapons defense company. The PatriotOne offers what they call, a Cognitive Microwave Radar (CMR) which is capable of scanning patterns and comparing them to that of weapon patterns that have been uploaded into the system. With each scan, the CMR is able to learn and remember patterns of scanned and confirmed weapons so that it will ultimately improve the accuracy of its system. In addition to the weapon recognition, when in need of other defense, the Iron Giant is also equipped with a high performance targeting system. While its system is high performance, it is not perfect. This is seen in the movie when Hogarth distracts the Iron Giant and it misses its shot on the battleship. In the living world, we are working on technology that is reasonably more accurate. TrackingPoint, a weapons company, has designed what the they call “Precision-Guided Firearms.” The company uses a fire control system called TriggerLink that virtually eliminates human error caused by misaiming, mistiming, and nervous system jitters. The system recognizes the object that you want to shoot creating a crosshair for you to lock on to. When you lock on, the system uses precise measurements to snap your crosshairs to the point of impact.

Iron Giant: Swarm Robotics

The Iron Giant has shown multiple accounts of breaking up into smaller components of itself. Each piece capable of acting as its own, This phenomenon is known as swarm robotics. There are two systems that are used by a swarm: group independence and hierarchy. Group independence is an action similar to that found in ant colonies where no one entity has authority over another, but they work together to complete an objective bigger than themselves. Group independence has the benefit where a swarm of this type can adapt to a change in their numbers. On the other hand, a hierarchy swarm has a queen bot that commands lesser robots called workers. These workers in turn command lesser robots called scouts which carry a lot of the labor. This is very similar to that found in the Iron Giant. Its head acts as a queen, signaling the other parts to return. Large components such as hands and legs are complicated and act as their own capable entity. It can be inferred that these are the worker bots, while smaller pieces such as his screw are scouts. There are two methods that can affect a swarms integrity: invasive robots and targeted takedowns. Group independence has the downside of being vulnerable by introducing an invasive robot. By taking a member of the swarm and reprogramming it, its false information and objectives can cause the swarm to fail as a whole because of the easy assimilation. Hierarchy does not suffer from this because it has tiers of authority however, by taking out just the queen robot, the swarm is shut down entirely. This was not attempted in the film, but the possibility is still there.

Iron Giant: Computational Power

When it comes to running complicated systems such as machine learning and AI, there is a heavy emphasis on required components to make them operate correctly. Semiconductors such as CPUs and GPUs are essential for these tasks. GPUs run data through multiple cores in parallel which is very useful at calculating large identical matrix multiplication in machine learning. Assuming that the Iron Giant is as intelligent as a human (given that he shows creativity, adaptability, emotions, understanding, and more humanlike qualities) it would take an enormous amount of power to run its “brain”. For reference In 2013, it took 40 minutes for the K Computer, which runs at about 10.5 petaflops per second, to simulate a second of human brain activity. Just to run and cool this machine required about 12.6 megawatts of electricity. This requires a mid size solar power plant station just to supply this.

Sputnik

Built and launched by the Soviet Union in 1957, Sputnik was the first manmade satellite to orbit the Earth. It did so for about two months, broadcasting radio signals. This caused a significant amount of distress in the United States, since the country did not have the technology to perform a similar feat. Additionally, the satellite’s multiple crossings over the United States and radio transmissions caused both citizens and the government to fear that Soviet Russia might be able to create a satellite to spy on them. Computer Guided Nuclear Missiles

In the movie, the military calls in the launch of a guided nuclear missile from a submarine to attack the Iron Giant. The technology to perform this feat has existed since 1955, which was the first deployment of the Regulus-1, the United States’ first functional cruise missile. (A cruise missile is any computer guided missile that flies through the low atmosphere to hit a grounded target). The Regulus was in service on aircraft carriers and submarines from 1955 to 1964, so it is likely (given that The Iron Giant takes place in 1957), that the Regulus is the missile depicted.
